About

The writer
behind the words.

I'm Shivasri — a BTech Computer Science student specialising in AI, and a LinkedIn ghostwriter for startup founders and tech builders.

I started writing because I noticed a pattern: the most interesting founders — the ones with real stories, hard lessons, and genuine insight — were invisible on LinkedIn. Not because they had nothing to say, but because they had no time to say it.

That's the gap I fill. I study how you think, interview you about your journey, and translate it into posts that sound exactly like you — just sharper, more structured, and actually published.

Being a student in AI gives me a genuine edge: I understand the founder and tech landscape from the inside, and I use AI tools to work faster without sacrificing the human voice that makes LinkedIn content actually work.

Writing Principles
01
Story before advice
Every great post starts with a real experience. Generic advice gets scrolled past. Stories get shared.
02
Hooks that earn the click
The first line is everything. If it doesn't stop the scroll, nothing else matters.
03
Conversational, not corporate
Short sentences. Real language. Posts that feel like a message from a smart friend, not a press release.
04
Ends with a conversation
Every post closes with a takeaway or question that invites people in. Comments = reach = growth.

Startup Lessons · Product–Market Fit
I spent $20,000 building a product nobody wanted
The problem wasn't the engineering. It was my ego. For six months, I stayed in "stealth mode." Polishing features. Perfecting the UI. Convincing myself we were building something revolutionary. Launch day came. And nothing happened. No signups. No excitement. Just silence. That's when it hit me: I hadn't built a solution. I'd built a monument to my own assumptions. Product–market fit isn't discovered behind a keyboard. It's discovered in messy conversations with real users. The ones most founders avoid. The lesson: → Validate the pain before writing a single line of code → If you can't sell the idea on a whiteboard, the app won't save you → Fall in love with the problem, not your first solution Because if you're not talking to users every week… You're not building a startup. You're just expensive daydreaming. What's the most expensive lesson your career has taught you?
Hiring & Team Building
I spent $200,000 learning a hiring lesson I could've learned from a $20 book.
In the early days of my startup, I thought hiring fast meant we were growing fast. More people. More momentum. More progress. At least that's what I told myself. So I hired quickly. Great resumes. Big company backgrounds. Impressive LinkedIn profiles. Three months later, the cracks started showing. Communication slowed down. Product velocity dropped. And I was spending most of my time managing conflicts instead of building the company. Eventually, I had to let three "star hires" go in the same week. It was brutal. But it taught me something every founder eventually learns: A B-player in a startup isn't neutral. They're a liability. The lesson: → Hire for hunger, not just pedigree → If it's not a "Hell Yes," it's a No → One A-player will outperform five average hires Move fast with your product. But move slow with your team. Your runway will thank you. What's a hiring lesson you learned the hard way?
AI & The Future of Work
I stopped hiring for "experience." Now I hire for speed of learning.
Six months ago, I thought AI would just be a fancy spellcheck for my team. I was wrong. One of our junior developers used LLMs to ship a feature in two days. A feature that used to take our senior lead almost two weeks. The difference wasn't intelligence. It was mindset. The senior engineer was stuck doing things the "old way." The junior engineer treated AI like a tireless co-founder. That's when it hit me. AI isn't going to replace your job. But someone who knows how to leverage AI properly absolutely might. The real competitive advantage isn't what you know anymore. It's how fast you can learn tools that didn't exist yesterday. The takeaway: → Stop hiring only for years of experience → Look for people obsessed with learning new tools → In fast industries, adaptability beats expertise Because in a world moving this fast… The winners won't be the most experienced. They'll be the most adaptable. Is your team actually saving time with AI — or just using it to write better emails?
Founder Productivity
I spent three years working 14-hour days… and stayed in the exact same place.
My calendar was full. Back-to-back Zoom calls. Endless Slack notifications. "Quick syncs" that somehow lasted an hour. I felt busy every minute of the day. Exhausted. Stressed. Always "on." But something didn't add up. Revenue wasn't moving. The product wasn't improving. That's when I realized something uncomfortable. I wasn't productive. I was just busy. Founders often confuse motion with progress. We fill our days with meetings because it feels productive to be needed by everyone. But the most valuable thing a founder can do is actually the opposite. Be unavailable. If your entire day is reactive… You're not leading. You're just a very expensive firefighter. So I changed one rule. The first four hours of my day are protected. No Slack. No meetings. No "quick calls." Just deep work. Because one hour of focused building will always beat ten hours of busywork. Are you actually moving the needle today… or just clearing your inbox?
Building in Public
The moment I stopped hiding the messy middle… my startup actually started growing.
For the first year, I only shared the polished wins. Funding updates. Perfect product screenshots. Milestone hires. It all looked impressive. But nobody cared. Then one Sunday, our server crashed. For six hours. Customers couldn't log in. The team was panicking. Slack was chaos. Instead of hiding it, I posted about it. I shared what went wrong. The frantic debugging. And the lesson we learned about redundancy. That post did more for our brand than a year of "perfect" updates. My inbox filled with messages. Not criticism. Other founders saying, "Been there." And potential customers saying they trusted us because we were honest. That's when I understood something important. Building in public isn't about bragging. It's about building trust. When people see how you solve problems, they don't just buy your product. They start rooting for you. The takeaway: → Transparency builds credibility → People connect with the journey, not just the wins → Your process is more interesting than your highlights Stop waiting for the perfect update. Share the journey instead. Are you building in silence… or bringing your audience along for the ride?
